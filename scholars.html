Scholars & Experts

The historians, linguists, philosophers, and indigenous academics who have shaped our understanding of Nahua civilization across five centuries of scholarship.

Profiles of scholars and experts in Nahua studies
📜
1499 – 1590

Fray Bernardino de Sahagún

Franciscan Friar · Father of Mesoamerican Ethnography

Bernardino de Sahagún was a Franciscan friar from Spain who arrived in New Spain in 1529 and dedicated more than sixty years of his life to documenting Nahua civilization with a depth and rigor that has no parallel in the colonial Americas. His magnum opus, the Historia General de las Cosas de Nueva España—known as the Florentine Codex for the Biblioteca Medicea Laurenziana in Florence where the manuscript resides—is the single most important ethnographic document of the Nahua world.

Beginning in the 1540s, Sahagún developed a systematic methodology that anticipated modern ethnographic practice by centuries. He assembled groups of elderly Nahua noblemen and scholars at sites in Tepepulco, Tlatelolco, and Mexico City, conducting extended interviews using structured questionnaires. His trilingual Nahua assistants—graduates of the Colegio de Santa Cruz de Tlatelolco, including Antonio Valeriano, Alonso Vegerano, and Martín Jacobita—transcribed oral testimony into written Nahuatl and translated key passages into Spanish.

The resulting twelve-book encyclopedia runs to over 2,400 pages with nearly 2,000 watercolor illustrations and covers every dimension of Nahua life: cosmology, theology, calendar systems, natural history, medicine, social organization, education, warfare, trade, crafts, rhetoric, poetry, and daily customs. Book VI, containing the huehuehtlahtolli ("speeches of the elders"), is an irreplaceable record of Nahua moral philosophy and rhetorical art.

Modern scholarship increasingly recognizes the Florentine Codex as a collaborative and primarily indigenous intellectual achievement. The Nahua informants were not passive subjects but active scholars who shaped the questions, organized the material, and composed the Nahuatl text. Sahagún's genius lay in creating the framework that enabled this indigenous scholarship to be preserved in written form.

Key Works

Historia General de las Cosas de Nueva España (Florentine Codex, 12 books, c. 1545–1590); Psalmodia Christiana (1583); Primeros Memoriales (c. 1558–1561). The Florentine Codex has been published in full critical editions by Arthur J.O. Anderson and Charles Dibble (1950–1982) and more recently in a digital edition by the Getty Research Institute.

📚
1926 – 2019

Miguel León-Portilla

Philosopher · Nahuatl Scholar · Champion of Indigenous Intellectual Traditions

Miguel León-Portilla was the most influential scholar of Nahua civilization in the twentieth century and the person most responsible for bringing Nahua intellectual achievements to global attention. A philosopher by training at the Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México (UNAM), where he studied under the Nahuatl specialist Ángel María Garibay K., León-Portilla devoted his entire career to demonstrating that the Nahua peoples possessed a genuine philosophical tradition comparable to those of ancient Greece, India, and China.

His 1956 doctoral dissertation, published as La filosofía náhuatl estudiada en sus fuentes (English translation: Aztec Thought and Culture, 1963), was a landmark work that single-handedly transformed the field. Drawing on the Nahuatl texts preserved in the Florentine Codex, the Cantares Mexicanos, and the Romances de los Señores de la Nueva España, León-Portilla reconstructed the philosophical thought of the tlamatinimeh (philosopher-sages), demonstrating their sophisticated engagement with questions of truth, beauty, morality, the nature of the divine, and the meaning of human existence.

His second major work, Visión de los vencidos (The Broken Spears, 1959), assembled indigenous accounts of the Spanish conquest, presenting for the first time a coherent narrative of the fall of Tenochtitlan from the Nahua perspective. This book, translated into dozens of languages, revolutionized popular understanding of the conquest by challenging the Eurocentric narrative that had dominated for four centuries.

León-Portilla spent over six decades at UNAM, where he directed the Institute of Historical Research and trained generations of Nahuatl scholars. He was a tireless advocate for indigenous language rights, serving as Mexican ambassador to UNESCO and championing the recognition of Nahuatl as a language of scholarship and literary expression. He published over fifty books and hundreds of articles spanning pre-Columbian philosophy, colonial history, Nahuatl literature, and contemporary indigenous rights.

Essential Reading

Aztec Thought and Culture (1963); The Broken Spears: The Aztec Account of the Conquest of Mexico (1962); Pre-Columbian Literatures of Mexico (1969); Fifteen Poets of the Aztec World (1992); Bernardino de Sahagún: First Anthropologist (2002). His work remains the essential starting point for anyone studying Nahua intellectual history.

1892 – 1967

Ángel María Garibay Kintana

Priest · Philologist · Pioneer of Nahuatl Literary Studies

Ángel María Garibay K. was the Mexican priest and philologist who, more than any other individual, established the academic study of Nahuatl literature as a serious scholarly discipline. A Catholic priest who served in rural Nahua communities in the Valley of Toluca, Garibay learned to speak Nahuatl fluently from his parishioners and developed an intimate knowledge of the living language that informed all his subsequent scholarship.

His monumental Historia de la literatura náhuatl (1953–1954), a two-volume work spanning over 1,500 pages, was the first comprehensive literary history of the Nahuatl tradition, covering the full range of genres from sacred hymns and philosophical poetry to historical chronicles, oratory, and dramatic performance. He also produced critical editions and Spanish translations of the two most important collections of Nahuatl poetry: the Cantares Mexicanos and the Romances de los Señores de la Nueva España.

As a professor at UNAM, Garibay trained the next generation of Nahuatl scholars, most notably Miguel León-Portilla, who would carry his teacher's project to an international audience. Garibay's insistence that Nahuatl literature deserved to be studied with the same philological rigor applied to Greek, Latin, and Sanskrit texts was revolutionary for its time and laid the groundwork for the flowering of Nahua studies in the second half of the twentieth century.

Key Works

Historia de la literatura náhuatl (2 vols., 1953–1954); Llave del náhuatl (1940); Poesia náhuatl (3 vols., 1964–1968); Épica náhuatl (1945). His editions and translations remain fundamental reference works for students of Nahuatl literature.

1936 – 2021

Alfredo López Austin

Historian · Anthropologist · Authority on Nahua Religion & Cosmovision

Alfredo López Austin was one of the most original and influential scholars of Mesoamerican religion and worldview. A historian and anthropologist at UNAM, he devoted his career to reconstructing the deepest layers of Nahua cosmological thought—the concepts of body, soul, illness, the sacred, and the structure of the cosmos that underlay all aspects of Nahua life.

His masterwork, Cuerpo humano e ideología: Las concepciones de los antiguos nahuas (1980; English translation: The Human Body and Ideology: Concepts of the Ancient Nahuas, 1988), transformed the study of Mesoamerican religion. Drawing on a meticulous analysis of Nahuatl terminology in the Florentine Codex and other colonial sources, López Austin reconstructed the Nahua understanding of the human body as a vessel containing three principal animistic entities: the tonalli (located in the head, associated with solar heat and individual fate), the teyolia (in the heart, the seat of consciousness and vitality), and the ihiyotl (in the liver, connected to passion, desire, and nocturnal emanations).

His later works, including Tamoanchan y Tlalocan (1994) and Los mitos del tlacuache (1990; The Myths of the Opossum, 1993), developed the concept of a deep Mesoamerican cosmological structure—a "hard nucleus" (núcleo duro) of shared beliefs that persisted across cultures and centuries, from the Olmec through the Nahua. This framework has become one of the most productive theoretical tools in Mesoamerican studies.

Essential Reading

The Human Body and Ideology (1988); The Myths of the Opossum (1993); Tamoanchan, Tlalocan: Places of Mist (1997); The Rabbit on the Face of the Moon (1996), with Leonardo López Luján: Monte Sagrado/Templo Mayor (2009). His concept of the núcleo duro of Mesoamerican cosmovision remains indispensable.

1933 – 2014

James Lockhart

Historian · Founder of the New Philology · Master of Nahuatl Documentary Analysis

James Lockhart, professor of history at UCLA, fundamentally transformed the study of colonial Latin America by pioneering the "New Philology"—an approach that uses mundane documents written in indigenous languages (wills, land records, municipal proceedings, parish registers) rather than Spanish-language chronicles to reconstruct the social history of colonized peoples from the inside. His insistence on reading Nahuatl sources in the original language, rather than relying on Spanish translations, revealed a world of indigenous agency, adaptation, and cultural resilience that had been invisible to previous generations of scholars.

His masterwork, The Nahuas After the Conquest: A Social and Cultural History of the Indians of Central Mexico, Sixteenth Through Eighteenth Centuries (1992), is arguably the most important single book ever written about Nahua civilization in the colonial period. Drawing on thousands of Nahuatl-language documents from archives across Mexico, Lockhart demonstrated that the Nahua did not simply submit to Spanish colonialism but engaged in a complex, centuries-long process of selective adaptation that he called "Double Mistaken Identity"—a dynamic in which both Spaniards and Nahuas interpreted each other's institutions through the lens of their own cultural categories, often reaching workable but fundamentally different understandings of the same arrangements.

Lockhart trained dozens of doctoral students at UCLA who became leading scholars in their own right, creating a school of Nahuatl-based colonial history that has produced an extraordinary body of scholarship. His insistence on linguistic rigor—that historians must learn to read Nahuatl before making claims about Nahua society—raised the standard of evidence in the field and ensured that indigenous voices would be heard in their own words.

Essential Reading

The Nahuas After the Conquest (1992); We People Here: Nahuatl Accounts of the Conquest of Mexico (1993); Nahuatl as Written (2001); with Frances Karttunen: Nahuatl in the Middle Years (1976). His three-stage model of Nahua linguistic and cultural change remains a foundational framework for colonial Mesoamerican studies.

📘

Camilla Townsend

Historian · Rutgers University · Bancroft Prize Winner

Camilla Townsend, Distinguished Professor of History at Rutgers University, has emerged as one of the most important historians of the Nahua world in the twenty-first century. Her 2019 book Fifth Sun: A New History of the Aztecs, which won the Cundill History Prize, accomplished something remarkable: it retold the entire history of the Mexica people from their own perspective, drawing primarily on Nahuatl-language sources rather than Spanish chronicles.

Townsend's approach builds on Lockhart's New Philology but extends it into narrative history accessible to a general audience. Fifth Sun follows the Mexica from their migrations into the Valley of Mexico through the height of the Triple Alliance to the colonial aftermath, centering indigenous voices at every stage. She demonstrates that the Nahuas were sophisticated political actors who understood their world clearly—not the superstitious, Quetzalcoatl-believing people of colonial mythology.

Her earlier work, Malintzin's Choices: An Indian Woman in the Conquest of Mexico (2006), offered a groundbreaking reinterpretation of the woman known as La Malinche or Doña Marina, the Nahua-Maya interpreter who became Cortés's translator and advisor. By situating Malintzin within the context of Nahua gender relations, slavery, and political strategy, Townsend transformed a figure traditionally vilified in Mexican national mythology into a complex human being navigating impossible circumstances with remarkable intelligence.

Her most recent work, Annals of Native America: How the Nahuas of Colonial Mexico Kept Their History Alive (2017), traces the extraordinary tradition of Nahuatl-language annals writing that continued for centuries after the conquest, demonstrating the persistence of indigenous historical consciousness under colonial rule.

Essential Reading

Fifth Sun: A New History of the Aztecs (2019, Cundill Prize); Malintzin's Choices: An Indian Woman in the Conquest of Mexico (2006); Annals of Native America (2017). Townsend's work represents the cutting edge of Nahua historical scholarship.

📖

Matthew Restall

Historian · Penn State University · Debunker of Conquest Myths

Matthew Restall, Edwin Erle Sparks Professor of Colonial Latin American History at Penn State University, has been instrumental in dismantling the myths that have distorted popular understanding of the Spanish conquest for five centuries. His 2003 book Seven Myths of the Spanish Conquest systematically debunked the most persistent misconceptions: that a handful of Spaniards conquered millions through superior civilization, that indigenous peoples were paralyzed by the belief that Europeans were gods, that the conquest was a unique event rather than a long process, and that indigenous cultures were destroyed rather than transformed.

The myth Restall has done most to dismantle is the "Quetzalcoatl myth"—the widely repeated claim that Moctezuma II believed Cortés was the returning god Quetzalcoatl. Working alongside scholars such as Camilla Townsend and Susan Gillespie, Restall demonstrated that this narrative was a post-conquest fabrication, assembled from colonial-era sources that served Spanish political interests by portraying the conquest as divinely ordained and the Mexica as passive recipients of their own destruction.

His broader body of work, including When Montezuma Met Cortés: The True Story of the Meeting that Changed History (2018), has emphasized the agency, intelligence, and political sophistication of indigenous actors during the conquest period, challenging narratives that reduce the fall of the Mexica Empire to a story of European technological and cultural superiority.

Essential Reading

Seven Myths of the Spanish Conquest (2003); When Montezuma Met Cortés (2018); with Kris Lane: Latin America in Colonial Times (2011). His work is essential for anyone seeking to understand the conquest beyond the myths.

📰

Louise M. Burkhart

Ethnohistorian · University at Albany · Scholar of Nahua Christianity

Louise Burkhart, professor of anthropology at the University at Albany (SUNY), is the leading scholar of the encounter between Nahua religion and Christianity in the colonial period. Her groundbreaking book The Slippery Earth: Nahua-Christian Moral Dialogue in Sixteenth-Century Mexico (1989) demonstrated that the conversion of the Nahuas to Christianity was not the straightforward replacement of one belief system by another but a complex, creative process of cultural translation in which Nahua concepts fundamentally reshaped the Christianity that emerged in colonial Mexico.

Burkhart showed that when Franciscan missionaries translated Christian moral concepts into Nahuatl, the resulting texts carried meanings that their European authors did not intend. The Nahuatl word tlatlacolli, used to translate "sin," actually meant something closer to "damage" or "spoiled things"—a concept rooted in Nahua ideas about cosmic balance rather than Christian notions of moral transgression against a divine lawgiver. Similarly, Nahua concepts of "the good life" (qualli nemiliztli) emphasized moderation, social responsibility, and cosmic harmony in ways that overlapped with but also significantly diverged from Christian virtue ethics.

Her later work on Nahua religious theater, including her critical editions of colonial Nahuatl-language plays, has revealed a vibrant tradition of dramatic performance through which Nahua communities negotiated the meanings of Christianity on their own terms, incorporating indigenous aesthetic sensibilities, humor, and social commentary into ostensibly Christian dramatic forms.

Essential Reading

The Slippery Earth: Nahua-Christian Moral Dialogue in Sixteenth-Century Mexico (1989); Holy Wednesday: A Nahua Drama from Early Colonial Mexico (1996); Before Guadalupe: The Virgin Mary in Early Colonial Nahuatl Literature (2001). Her work is essential for understanding colonial-era Nahua religious thought.

Frances Karttunen

Linguist · University of Texas at Austin · Nahuatl Lexicographer

Frances Karttunen is the linguist who created the standard reference dictionary of Classical Nahuatl used by virtually every scholar working with Nahuatl-language texts today. Her An Analytical Dictionary of Nahuatl (1983), developed in collaboration with James Lockhart, applies rigorous linguistic methodology to the analysis of Nahuatl vocabulary, providing detailed etymological information, morphological analysis, and attestations from primary sources for thousands of lexical entries.

Unlike earlier Nahuatl dictionaries that were essentially word lists compiled by colonial missionaries, Karttunen's dictionary treats Nahuatl as a language deserving the same systematic linguistic analysis applied to any other language. Her work revealed the extraordinary morphological complexity and expressive precision of Nahuatl, demonstrating how the language's agglutinative structure allows the construction of words that encode complex relationships and abstract concepts in ways that defy simple translation into European languages.

Beyond her lexicographic work, Karttunen has made important contributions to the study of language contact and bilingualism in colonial Mexico, and her book Between Worlds: Interpreters, Guides, and Survivors (1994) examined the lives of cultural intermediaries—including Malintzin—who navigated between indigenous and European worlds during the age of European expansion.

Essential Reading

An Analytical Dictionary of Nahuatl (1983); with James Lockhart: Nahuatl in the Middle Years (1976); Between Worlds: Interpreters, Guides, and Survivors (1994). Her dictionary is an indispensable tool for any serious engagement with Nahuatl texts.

James Maffie

Philosopher · University of Maryland · Systematizer of Aztec Philosophy

James Maffie, professor of philosophy at the University of Maryland, is the author of what many consider the most important work of Nahua philosophy since León-Portilla: Aztec Philosophy: Understanding a World in Motion (2014). Where León-Portilla presented Nahua thought primarily through its poetic and literary expressions, Maffie undertakes a systematic philosophical reconstruction, arguing that Nahua metaphysics constitutes a coherent and sophisticated philosophical system organized around the concept of teotl—a dynamic, self-generating, sacred cosmic energy.

Maffie argues that Nahua metaphysics is fundamentally processual: reality is not composed of static substances (as in Western metaphysics from Aristotle onward) but of dynamic, ever-changing processes. Teotl is not a being or a god in the Western sense but the ceaseless creative-destructive energy that constitutes all of reality. The familiar Nahua deities—Quetzalcoatl, Tezcatlipoca, Tlaloc, Huitzilopochtli—are not independent beings but aspects, phases, or manifestations of this single underlying reality.

This processual metaphysics generates a distinctive epistemology (knowledge comes through embodied practice, not abstract contemplation), ethics (the good life consists of maintaining balance and "walking in balance" on the "slippery earth"), and aesthetics (art is a practice of "making things well-rooted" rather than representing an independent reality). Maffie's work demonstrates that Nahua thought is not merely a collection of myths and beliefs but a rigorous philosophical framework that deserves a place alongside the great philosophical traditions of the world.

Essential Reading

Aztec Philosophy: Understanding a World in Motion (2014). This 600-page work is the most comprehensive philosophical treatment of Nahua metaphysics, epistemology, and ethics ever published. It should be read alongside León-Portilla's Aztec Thought and Culture for a complete picture of Nahua philosophical achievement.

📝

R. Joe Campbell

Linguist · Indiana University · Nahuatl Morphological Analysis

R. Joe Campbell, professor emeritus at Indiana University, made foundational contributions to the formal linguistic analysis of Nahuatl and to making Nahuatl texts accessible to scholars through systematic morphological analysis. His work on the Florentine Codex—particularly his concordance and morphological database of the Nahuatl text—created tools that have enabled generations of researchers to work productively with this vast and complex document.

Campbell's approach to Nahuatl was rigorously structural, treating the language with the same formal precision that linguists apply to any other language. His analyses of Nahuatl verb morphology revealed the extraordinary expressive power of the Nahuatl verbal system, in which a single verb form can encode information about subject, object, direction, causation, applicative relationships, and aspectual distinctions that would require entire clauses in English.

Through his teaching at Indiana University, Campbell trained many of the linguists and philologists who now work with Nahuatl texts in the United States. His commitment to making Nahuatl accessible through pedagogical materials and linguistic tools has had a lasting impact on the field.

📄

Gordon Whittaker

Linguist · University of Göttingen · Nahuatl Writing Systems Expert

Gordon Whittaker, professor at the University of Göttingen in Germany, is the leading international authority on the Nahuatl writing system. His work has demonstrated that the pre-conquest Nahua writing system was far more sophisticated than previously recognized—not merely "pictographic" (recording ideas through pictures) but a complex system that combined logographic, syllabic, and phonetic elements capable of encoding specific Nahuatl words and grammatical structures.

Whittaker's detailed analyses of place-name glyphs, personal-name signs, and calendrical notations in pre-conquest and early colonial codices have revealed that Nahua scribes employed a range of writing strategies including rebuses, phonetic complements, semantic determinatives, and syllabic signs that parallel strategies found in other ancient writing systems such as Egyptian hieroglyphs, Sumerian cuneiform, and Maya script.

His work challenges the longstanding assumption that the Nahua writing system was fundamentally different from (and inferior to) the "true writing" systems of the Old World, demonstrating instead that it occupied a position on the same continuum of writing development and was evolving toward greater phoneticism at the time of the Spanish conquest.

Key Contributions

Whittaker's decipherments of Nahua script signs have appeared in numerous articles in journals such as Ancient Mesoamerica and Mexicon. His comprehensive treatment Deciphering Aztec Hieroglyphs (2021) is the definitive work on the subject.

Indigenous Nahuatl-Speaking Scholars

Native-speaker academics reshaping Nahua studies from within

One of the most significant developments in Nahua studies in recent decades has been the emergence of indigenous Nahuatl-speaking scholars who bring native-speaker competence and lived cultural experience to the academic study of their own traditions. These scholars are transforming the field by challenging the assumptions of non-indigenous researchers, offering new interpretations grounded in intimate knowledge of the language, and centering indigenous perspectives in academic discourse.

At institutions across Mexico—including UNAM, the Benemérita Universidad Autónoma de Puebla (BUAP), the Universidad Veracruzana, and the Centro de Investigaciones y Estudios Superiores en Antropología Social (CIESAS)—indigenous scholars are producing new research on Nahuatl dialectology, ethnopoetics, traditional ecological knowledge, indigenous governance systems, and the relationship between colonial-era texts and contemporary community practices. Their work bridges the artificial divide between "historical" and "contemporary" Nahua studies, demonstrating that the traditions documented in sixteenth-century sources remain living realities in twenty-first-century communities.

These scholars face unique challenges: navigating academic institutions that have historically marginalized indigenous knowledge, working in communities where academic research is sometimes viewed with suspicion due to histories of extractive scholarship, and balancing the demands of academic careers with responsibilities to their home communities. Their success in overcoming these obstacles is reshaping not only Nahua studies but the broader field of indigenous studies worldwide.

💻

Digital Humanities and Nahuatl Studies

Technology enabling new frontiers in the study and preservation of Nahua knowledge

The application of digital humanities methods to Nahua studies is opening new research possibilities and making Nahuatl-language sources accessible to wider audiences than ever before. Several major initiatives are at the forefront of this transformation.

The Digital Florentine Codex project, hosted by the Getty Research Institute, has made high-resolution images of the entire Florentine Codex freely available online alongside the Nahuatl text and Spanish and English translations, enabling scholars worldwide to work with this fundamental source without traveling to Florence. Similar digitization efforts at the Bibliothèque nationale de France, the Bodleian Library at Oxford, and Mexican archives have made colonial-era codices and manuscripts accessible to researchers and communities who were previously unable to consult the originals.

Computational linguistics is being applied to Nahuatl for the first time, with researchers developing tools for automatic morphological analysis, machine-assisted translation, and corpus analysis of large collections of Nahuatl texts. These tools promise to accelerate the pace of scholarly work and to reveal linguistic patterns that would be invisible to unaided human analysis.

Online pedagogical platforms have democratized access to Nahuatl language instruction, with university courses, community-based programs, and self-study materials available through websites, apps, and social media. The development of Nahuatl-language keyboards, spellcheckers, and text-to-speech systems is creating the digital infrastructure necessary for Nahuatl to function as a language of the digital age.

Major Research Centers and Programs

Where Nahua studies happens today

The study of Nahua civilization is sustained by a network of research centers, university programs, and scholarly organizations across the Americas and Europe. The following institutions represent the principal centers of Nahua studies today:

  • UNAM (Mexico City) — The Instituto de Investigaciones Históricas (IIH) and the Instituto de Investigaciones Antropológicas (IIA) at the Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México remain the world's leading centers for Nahua studies, housing the largest concentrations of Nahuatl-competent scholars and extensive archives of colonial and modern Nahuatl documents.
  • Yale University — The Yale Nahuatl language program, building on the legacy of scholars associated with Lockhart's New Philology, offers intensive Nahuatl instruction and supports research on colonial Nahuatl texts.
  • Tulane University — The Middle American Research Institute and the Latin American Library house major collections of Mesoamerican materials and support research on Nahua history and archaeology.
  • University of Göttingen (Germany) — A leading European center for Nahuatl linguistics and writing systems research.
  • INALI (Mexico) — The Instituto Nacional de Lenguas Indígenas supports the documentation, standardization, and revitalization of all Nahuatl varieties spoken in Mexico.
  • Templo Mayor Museum (Mexico City) — The ongoing excavation of the Templo Mayor, directed by Leonardo López Luján, continues to produce groundbreaking archaeological discoveries that illuminate Mexica religion, politics, and material culture.

International conferences, including the biennial meetings of the European Association of Mayanists (Wayeb) and the annual meetings of the American Society for Ethnohistory, provide regular forums for scholars to share new research and debate interpretations. The field is vibrant, interdisciplinary, and growing, with new generations of scholars drawn by the intellectual richness of Nahua civilization and the urgency of documenting and preserving indigenous knowledge.

Getting Started with Nahua Studies

For those interested in pursuing Nahua studies, the essential starting points are: (1) learning Nahuatl—even a basic understanding of the language transforms one's engagement with the sources; (2) reading the foundational works listed throughout this page; and (3) engaging with the scholarly community through conferences, seminars, and online forums. The field welcomes scholars from all disciplinary backgrounds—history, anthropology, linguistics, philosophy, art history, archaeology, and literature—reflecting the multidimensional richness of Nahua civilization itself.

Primary Sources

The Codices: Windows into Nahua Knowledge

The scholars profiled on this page have spent their careers deciphering, translating, and interpreting the extraordinary body of Nahua primary sources—from pre-conquest pictorial codices to colonial-era Nahuatl manuscripts to the living oral traditions of contemporary communities.

These sources represent one of the richest documentary traditions of any indigenous civilization in the Americas, and the work of interpreting them is far from complete. New discoveries continue to be made, new methods yield new insights, and new generations of scholars bring fresh perspectives to ancient questions.

  • Over 500 surviving pre-conquest and colonial codices
  • Thousands of Nahuatl-language municipal and legal documents
  • The Florentine Codex: 2,400+ pages of Nahua knowledge
  • Living oral traditions in hundreds of Nahua communities
